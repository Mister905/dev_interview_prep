Intro & Motivation

Elevator pitch

I’m a full-stack engineer with six years of experience building SaaS products end to end. I take ownership from requirements and design through implementation and production, which lets me connect technical architecture directly to real user impact.

Why are you interested in this role?

What are you looking for in your next role?

I’m looking for a role where I can continue to develop professionally with meaningful projects and really take ownership of my work. I also value being part of a team that works hard but still makes time to collaborate and have a good time together.

What does professional development mean to you?

I grow most by working on problems that push my technical boundaries and force me to learn through real-world implementation. I also see collaboration as a core part of development. Architecture discussions and code reviews are where I pick up new perspectives and help keep the team’s standards high.

Why did you choose software development?

I enjoyed the analytical side of my studies, but I realized I was more excited by actually building things. I taught myself to code, moved into a development role, and found that my research background helps a lot when I’m digging into complex requirements.

Why did you leave your last role?

I had reached a point where I was ready for a new challenge, and my role was later impacted by budget-related changes. Since then, I’ve been doing some freelance consulting while looking for my next full-time role.

What consulting work have you done?

I’m helping a friend validate a business idea by acting as a technical consultant. I’m responsible for the architecture and for building a prototype using React, TypeScript, and Node. The product tracks how Airbnb listings change over time by capturing daily snapshots of things like amenities and photos, keeping a full history so users can compare any two dates. I’ve focused on making the interface clean and responsive so the data is easy to explore. I’m also helping a friend who is a teacher build a trivia app based on historical Jeopardy data, giving fans a fun way to play and sharpen their knowledge.
Technical Stack & Experience

Describe your preferred stack

I’m most productive with React and Next.js on the frontend. For the backend, I usually go with Node, using either Express or NestJS depending on how much structure the project needs. I’m also comfortable jumping between SQL and NoSQL databases.

Describe your frontend experience

On the frontend, I mainly work with React and Next.js, with a focus on reusable components and clean state management. I typically choose Next.js for production because it handles routing and image optimization out of the box, but I’ll use a standard React setup for simpler internal tools where SEO is not a concern. I also prioritize accessibility and responsiveness using Tailwind and Shadcn. I like that stack because it avoids the overhead of a large component library. Since Shadcn components live directly in the codebase, I fully own and customize them, which makes layout changes easier and keeps the codebase lean.

Describe your backend experience

I build backends primarily with Node.js, using Express for lighter APIs and NestJS when I need a more structured and modular architecture. I’ve also worked with Flask, which has given me perspective on how different ecosystems approach routing and middleware. Regardless of the framework, my focus is on building robust REST APIs, handling authentication, and keeping data validation consistent throughout the system.

Do you prefer frontend or backend development?

I enjoy full-stack work because I like seeing how the architecture on both sides connects. I’m comfortable moving between frontend and backend and especially enjoy the problem solving that happens at the intersection. For me, it’s less about choosing a favorite layer and more about owning a feature from the database through to the UI.

Have you used [technology] before?

I haven't used [technology] professionally yet, but I’m a fast learner when it comes to new stacks. Since I have a strong grasp of the underlying patterns in my current stack, I’ve found that I can usually translate those concepts to new frameworks and get up to speed very quickly.
State Management / Redux

When should you use Redux vs local React state?

I use Redux for global state that multiple components need, like auth, theme, or app-wide preferences. For state that lives in a single component, I stick with useState or useReducer to keep things simple and avoid unnecessary re-renders. I use Redux Toolkit to reduce boilerplate and follow standard patterns.

What are best practices for structuring Redux in large apps?

In large apps, I structure Redux by splitting the store into feature slices, which keeps each domain manageable and easier to maintain, while the root reducer stays simple. I keep side-effects out of reducers and handle async logic separately. I use memoized selectors to prevent unnecessary re-renders and only store state that truly needs to be shared, deriving everything else in selectors to keep the store clean.
API Design & System Architecture

How do you design APIs?

I follow RESTful principles with clear, resource-based naming and meaningful status codes so APIs are predictable and easy to use. For security, I use JWT authentication with roles embedded in the token, letting the API handle authorization without extra database lookups. I also focus on performance with pagination, filtering, and caching from the start. Consistent response structures ensure the frontend always knows what to expect, keeping development fast and predictable.

How do you structure large-scale React applications?

I organize code by feature, so each domain owns its components, hooks, and types, letting teams work in parallel and reducing the chance of conflicts. This also makes the codebase easier to navigate and scale as the app grows.

How do you design APIs for scalability and maintainability?

I design APIs with clear resource boundaries, consistent response shapes, and built-in features like pagination and filtering so the frontend can use them predictably and scale easily. I structure the backend modularly, add logging and metrics early, and enforce conventions and shared types to keep the system maintainable as it grows.
Databases

SQL, ORMs, and database experience

I have strong experience with relational databases, designing schemas, managing indexes, and handling transactions. I use ORMs like Prisma for type safety and speed but can write raw SQL when needed for complex queries or optimizations. I typically use SQL for structured, integrity-critical data and NoSQL for flexible schemas or fast iteration.
Scalability & Performance

Cloud deployment & CI/CD experience

I use Docker to standardize my local environment once the project structure is in place. After defining the frontend and backend directories, I containerize them so the team is aligned on the same versions and configurations from the start. For deployment, I typically use AWS, with S3 for the frontend and Lambda for the backend, and I set up CI/CD pipelines to automate testing and versioned releases. I focus on predictability and always plan for rollbacks so a known good state can be restored quickly if issues arise.

Git experience

I use Git daily following a feature-branch workflow. I maintain a clean, linear history by squashing incremental updates into meaningful commits and rebasing to keep my branch current. This helps avoid messy merge commits and makes the project history easier to audit. I treat pull requests as more than just code checks. They are opportunities for peer review and knowledge sharing. By using clear naming conventions and descriptive PRs, I make sure the team understands the reasoning behind each change, which makes it easier for others to work in the codebase later.

How do you optimize React for large datasets?

I optimize React for large datasets by rendering only what’s visible and avoiding storing huge lists in global state. I fetch or stream data in chunks, and I memoize components so only what actually changes re-renders. This keeps the UI fast and responsive even with tens of thousands of items.

How do you handle API performance under heavy load?

I handle API performance under heavy load by building in pagination, caching, and rate limiting, which controls how many requests a client can make in a given time to protect the system. On the backend, I optimize queries, use batching or joins to avoid repeated lookups, and handle heavy operations asynchronously. I monitor performance with logging and metrics so I can spot bottlenecks, add indexes or caching where needed, and prevent slowdowns before they affect users.
UX/UI & Frontend Practices

UX/UI mindset and examples

I focus on UI and UX with a user-first mindset, building interfaces that are intuitive, accessible, and easy to navigate. I make sure apps are responsive and optimized for different mobile devices, and I provide clear feedback during loading or other interactions, using semantic HTML and ARIA roles to support accessibility. I also use modular component libraries to keep designs consistent and scalable.
Testing, Observability & Reliability

Testing strategies

I follow a multi-layered testing strategy using Jest for unit tests and integration tests to verify service interactions. I treat API validation as a priority, using Postman to simulate real-world scenarios. Beyond automated suites, I'm highly comfortable with manual data debugging when troubleshooting production edge cases. I use raw SQL to investigate state-dependent issues, ensuring I can identify the root cause, reproduce it in a safe environment, and ultimately lock in a fix with a new regression test.

Debugging production issues

I tackle production issues by starting with logs and monitoring to spot patterns missing in development. I isolate the failing component, reproduce the issue in a safe environment, and fix the root cause. I also add durable improvements like tests or validation to prevent the same problem from recurring.

How do you monitor production frontend apps?

I monitor production frontend apps using error tracking and real user monitoring to catch issues users actually experience. I track key performance metrics and set up alerts for regressions. I also focus on critical user flows, monitoring their success and speed so we can quickly trace and fix any problems that slip past staging.

What testing strategies do you use for complex React applications?

I use a layered testing approach. Unit tests cover pure logic and reusable functions, while component tests focus on user-facing behavior. I prioritize critical paths and edge cases to keep tests fast and meaningful. For integration, I test key flows that cross components or API boundaries, focusing on the areas that would have the biggest impact if they broke.
Leadership, Mentorship & Staff Skills

Complex Full Stack Project Leadership

At Civix, I led the full-stack development of a document submission and approval system for the NJ Office of Emergency Management. The biggest challenge was FEMA compliance, requiring strict data validation and a multi-stage approval workflow. I built the frontend in React, backend in NestJS, and designed the SQL schema to enforce compliance at the data level. Working closely with QA and stakeholders, I turned a rigid regulatory process into an intuitive user experience, handling everything from architecture to production support.

How do you prioritize work when multiple tasks compete?

When tasks compete, I prioritize them by weighing their importance to the business against how quickly they need to be done. I specifically look for tasks that are holding up other team members and handle those first so the whole team can keep moving. I also believe in clear, early communication. If a major bug forces me to pause my current work, I let my manager and stakeholders know right away. This allows us to adjust the timeline together and ensures we stay focused on the most important work without any surprises.

How do you approach unclear requirements or ambiguous problems?

I start by clarifying the goal and the constraints. I ask what success looks like, who is affected, and what we can and cannot change. I then break the problem into smaller pieces and tackle the riskiest or most uncertain parts first, often with a spike or a small prototype so we learn quickly. I document assumptions and share them with product and stakeholders so we stay aligned. If the requirements keep changing, I focus on a minimal solution that delivers value and is easy to extend, rather than building for every possible future. I have used this approach on projects where the product was still being defined and we had to ship while the scope evolved.

How do you measure the success of your features or technical decisions?

I tie success to outcomes that matter to the business and the user. For a feature, that might be adoption, completion rate, or time on task. For a technical change, it might be reduced latency, fewer bugs, or faster deploys. I define these up front when possible so we know what we are optimizing for. I use the data we already have, like analytics, error rates, and performance metrics, and I avoid adding instrumentation everywhere. When we shipped a refactor of a critical flow, we tracked error rate and load time before and after and used that to confirm the change was safe and to justify future investments.

How do you work in a team?

I thrive in a collaborative environment where code reviews and architecture discussions are used to share knowledge and keep our standards consistent. I’m a big fan of pair programming for tackling complex problems, and I make it a priority to be available whenever a teammate needs a second pair of eyes. I also view feedback as a two-way street. I try to provide constructive suggestions that help the code improve, while staying open to different perspectives on my own work. Ultimately, I want to be the reliable partner who helps the team stay productive and builds a better product together.

Handling disagreements

When I encounter a technical disagreement, I start by listening to the other perspective to make sure I fully understand their concerns. I then explain my own reasoning, focusing on the trade-offs and how each choice affects the project goals. By keeping the conversation centered on the problem rather than the person, we can stay focused on the most effective solution. My priority is always to reach a consensus that keeps the team moving forward without compromising on quality or maintainability.

How do you mentor junior engineers?

I give junior engineers ownership of small, clear tasks so they can learn by doing. I pair on tricky parts, do code reviews that explain the why, and encourage them to propose solutions first. I share system and business context so they can make better decisions, helping them grow quickly in a safe environment.

How do you influence architecture and team standards?

I lead by example and by writing things down. I document decisions and trade-offs in ADRs or design docs so the team has a shared reference. I propose standards when I see repeated pain, and I get buy-in by showing the impact with a small pilot or proof of concept before we roll it out broadly. I also use code reviews and architecture discussions to align on patterns. I do not insist on my way; I frame options and trade-offs so the team can choose together. When we introduced a new testing strategy, I ran a short workshop and left runbooks and examples so everyone could adopt it over time.

How do you resolve conflicts in the team?

I focus on the problem, not the person. I listen to each perspective and make sure everyone feels heard. I then help the team compare options against our goals and constraints so we can choose a path that everyone can support, even if it is not everyone's first choice. When there is a technical disagreement, I suggest we prototype or spike both approaches when it is feasible so we can decide on data rather than opinion. If we are stuck, I am willing to make a call and own it, and we document the decision and revisit it later if needed.
Past Failures & Technical Weaknesses

Dealing with failure

I once deployed a database migration that caused downtime. I took full ownership and fixed it, then updated our process to require staging tests with production-sized data and added granular monitoring. I learned that a fix isn’t done until the system is improved to prevent it from happening again.

Addressing technical weakness

Early in my career, I often over-engineered solutions for future needs. Now I focus more on simple, maintainable code that solves the current problem. This keeps things clean and makes it easier to adapt when new requirements actually come up.
AI & Productivity Tools

How do you use AI in your workflow?

I use AI to speed up my workflow without replacing my judgment. I start with a clear brief, let the AI help plan and generate code, then review everything carefully. This keeps work consistent and lets me focus on complex architectural challenges.

How do you communicate technical trade-offs to product and design teams?

I focus on outcomes and trade-offs, not implementation details. I explain options clearly, use simple language or sketches, and make decisions collaboratively. Being transparent about risks and priorities builds trust and avoids surprises.

How do you drive technical vision without formal authority?

I build credibility by shipping and helping others succeed. I propose ideas with clear problems, options, and a recommended path, backed by data or a quick proof of concept. I start pilots or docs without waiting for permission and break vision into actionable steps the team can take now. This approach has led to tangible wins, like improving frontend performance through a targeted spike and rollout.
